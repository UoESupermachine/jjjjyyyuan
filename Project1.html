Superconducting Wind Turbine Generators

Revolutionizing wind energy with high power density superconducting technology

Project Overview

Royal Academy of Engineering Funded Research

Our research team at the University of Edinburgh is developing next-generation superconducting wind turbine generators with significantly increased power density. This Royal Academy of Engineering funded project aims to overcome size and weight constraints in conventional designs while improving energy conversion efficiency.

Key project objectives include:

  • Develop lightweight superconducting generators achieving 40% weight reduction compared to conventional technologies
  • Design and optimize cryogenic cooling systems for reliable operation in offshore environments
  • Create novel electromagnetic designs that maximize the benefits of superconducting materials
  • Demonstrate technical and economic feasibility for large-scale wind turbine applications

This innovative approach has the potential to significantly reduce the cost of wind energy and enable larger, more efficient turbines for offshore installations.

Key Innovations

⚡

High Power Density

Our superconducting generators feature a significant size reduction with outer diameter reduced from 5.4m to 4m, and an actual mass reduction of 24% compared to conventional generators, making them ideal for offshore wind applications.

❄️

Advanced Cryogenic Systems

Novel cryogenic cooling solutions designed specifically for the harsh marine environment ensure reliable operation with minimal maintenance requirements.

🔄

Improved Efficiency

Superconducting windings with near-zero resistance significantly reduce electrical losses, improving overall energy conversion efficiency and reducing operational costs.

Technical Details

Superconducting Materials

Our research utilizes high-temperature superconductors (HTS) that maintain zero electrical resistance at temperatures achievable with modern cryogenic cooling systems. The primary materials being investigated include:

  • YBCO (Yttrium Barium Copper Oxide) coated conductors
  • MgB₂ (Magnesium Diboride) superconducting wires
  • BSCCO (Bismuth Strontium Calcium Copper Oxide) tape conductors

These materials enable operation at temperatures between 20-77K, significantly reducing the cooling requirements compared to traditional low-temperature superconductors.

Generator Design

Our superconducting generator design incorporates several innovative features:

  • Axial flux topology with HTS coils in the rotor, offering compact form factor and improved cooling accessibility
  • Modular construction allowing for easier maintenance and component replacement
  • Novel flux pump technology for contactless excitation of superconducting field coils
  • Optimized electromagnetic design to maximize torque density
  • Parallel development of a non-superconducting CGEN prototype for validation, which has been successfully completed

The design targets a power density exceeding 136W/kg

Cooling System

The cryogenic cooling system is a critical component of our superconducting generator design:

  • Closed-cycle cryocoolers with helium as the working fluid
  • Redundant cooling paths to ensure reliability
  • Advanced thermal management with multi-stage cooling
  • Vacuum insulation techniques to minimize heat leakage
  • Monitoring and control systems for temperature stability

Our cooling design achieves a coefficient of performance (COP) significantly higher than conventional systems, reducing parasitic power losses.

Testing & Validation

Our comprehensive testing program includes:

  • Small-scale prototype testing in controlled laboratory environments
  • Specialized testing of superconducting coils under varying magnetic fields
  • Thermal cycling and accelerated aging tests
  • Full-scale prototype testing under simulated operational conditions
  • Finite element analysis validation of electromagnetic and mechanical performance

Testing results have validated our design approach, demonstrating power densities and efficiencies exceeding our initial targets.
